Orange County’s coronavirus metrics further ease since new year spike

By Ian Wheeler | iwheeler@scng.com | Orange County Register
PUBLISHED: February 9, 2021 at 5:38 p.m. | UPDATED: February 9, 2021 at 5:38 p.m.

Three of Orange County’s crucial coronavirus metrics trended downward again this week, rounding out a month of pandemic decline following unprecedented highs just after the new year.

From a staggering case rate of 78.8 cases per day per 100,000 residents on Jan. 12 – the highest of the pandemic so far – Orange County’s rate of new daily cases plummeted to 29.7 per 100,000 by Tuesday, Feb. 9, according to a state Department of Public Health update. Last week, the case rate was 39 per 100,000 residents.

Testing positivity – the share of swab and spit tests coming back positive – inched down to 9.4% from 10.9% last week.

The health equity metric – testing positivity among Orange County’s denser and harder-hit communities where access to health care is limited – dropped to 12.4% from 13.9% last week.

Consistent with tendencies following past holidays, however, public health experts fear Super Bowl watch parties last weekend could cause another bump in COVID-19 cases.

Though Orange County remains in the strictest purple tier of the state’s four-tier pandemic tracking system – limiting visitor capacities at many businesses and public places – dramatic improvements in its metrics have put the less-restrictive red tier on the horizon.

By Monday, Orange County’s 33 hospitals were treating half the coronavirus patients they were one month ago. Adult intensive care bed usage by COVID-19 patients, a central concern that led to a regional stay-at-home order from early December through late January, is declining more gradually.

Meanwhile, public and private coronavirus vaccination campaigns are gaining momentum.

About 409,000 doses (mostly first, but also starting to include second doses) of both the Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna vaccines had been administered to Orange County residents by Tuesday, mostly to frontline health care workers and seniors age 65 and older, according to the state health department.
